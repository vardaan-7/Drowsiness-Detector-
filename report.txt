1. INTRODUCTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The rapid growth of road infrastructure around the world has been accompanied by
a similarly rapid rise in road traffic accidents. Among the many contributing
factors, driver fatigue has been consistently identified as one of the most
dangerous. A driver who is drowsy experiences significant degradation in reaction
time, situational awareness, and decision-making ability — often without being
conscious of their own impairment. This phenomenon makes drowsy driving
particularly insidious because, unlike intoxication, it leaves no chemical trace
and no external warning sign until it is too late.

Computer vision, a branch of artificial intelligence concerned with enabling
machines to interpret visual data, has emerged as a powerful tool for addressing
this problem. Modern webcams are inexpensive and ubiquitous, and high-quality
pre-trained models for face detection and facial landmark localisation are freely
available. By combining these tools, it is possible to build a real-time system
that continuously monitors a driver's facial features and triggers an alarm the
moment signs of drowsiness are detected.

This project presents such a system: the AI-Based Driver Drowsiness Detection
System. It uses OpenCV for video capture and image processing, dlib for face
detection and 68-point facial landmark estimation, and a well-established metric
called the Eye Aspect Ratio (EAR) to quantitatively measure eye closure. It also
implements yawn detection using the Mouth Aspect Ratio (MAR), blink counting, and
event logging to a CSV file. All processing is done locally, in real time, using
only a standard USB or built-in webcam.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2. PROBLEM STATEMENT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Road accidents caused by drowsy driving represent both a public safety crisis and
a significant economic burden. According to the United States National Highway
Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA), drowsy driving is responsible for an
estimated 100,000 police-reported crashes annually, with approximately 1,550
fatalities and 71,000 injuries. Comparable statistics exist across India, Europe,
and other high-traffic regions.

Existing prevention strategies — rumble strips, lane-departure warning systems,
coffee breaks — are either passive or reactive. They either alert a driver after a
dangerous event has already begun (lane drift), or rely on the driver to
self-assess their own fatigue, which is unreliable. What is needed is a proactive
system that detects physiological signs of drowsiness before dangerous behaviour
manifests.

The specific problem this project addresses is: given a standard video stream
from a webcam mounted on the dashboard of a vehicle, can we detect in real time
whether the driver is becoming drowsy, and if so, alert them immediately?


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3. OBJECTIVES
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The primary objectives of this project are as follows:

  (a) To implement a real-time face detection pipeline using pre-trained models,
      eliminating the need for any custom dataset collection or model training.

  (b) To compute the Eye Aspect Ratio (EAR) from 68-point facial landmarks and
      use it as a quantitative measure of eye state (open vs. closed).

  (c) To detect drowsiness when eye closure is sustained beyond a configurable
      threshold duration and trigger an audible alarm.

  (d) To implement yawn detection using the Mouth Aspect Ratio (MAR) as an
      additional, complementary indicator of drowsiness.

  (e) To count blinks and log all significant events (drowsiness alerts, yawns,
      blinks) with timestamps to a CSV file for post-session analysis.

  (f) To build the system in a modular, clean, and well-documented manner that
      can be extended or maintained by any developer.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4. METHODOLOGY
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The methodology of this project is grounded in the landmark research of Soukupová
and Čech (2016), who introduced the Eye Aspect Ratio as a reliable, geometry-based
measure of eye openness. Their insight was that the ratio of vertical eye distances
to horizontal eye distance remains relatively stable when the eye is open and
drops sharply when the eye closes. This simple geometric measure turns out to be
robust across different skin tones, lighting conditions, and camera angles —
making it an ideal choice for a practical system.

The overall pipeline is as follows:

Step 1 — Video Acquisition: OpenCV's VideoCapture reads frames from the webcam at
the system's maximum frame rate (typically 25–30 fps on standard hardware).

Step 2 — Grayscale Conversion: Each frame is converted to grayscale to reduce
computational load. dlib's face detector operates equally well on grayscale images.

Step 3 — Face Detection: dlib's HOG (Histogram of Oriented Gradients) + SVM face
detector locates all human faces in the frame. This detector is fast enough for
real-time use on a CPU and is reliable under typical indoor lighting conditions.

Step 4 — Landmark Prediction: For each detected face, dlib's 68-point shape
predictor regresses the positions of 68 facial landmarks. These landmarks cover
the jaw, eyebrows, nose, eyes, and mouth, and are indexed consistently across
individuals.

Step 5 — EAR Computation: The six landmark points corresponding to each eye are
extracted (points 37–42 for the left eye and 43–48 for the right). The EAR is
computed as:

                 ||p2 - p6|| + ||p3 - p5||
        EAR  =  ───────────────────────────
                      2 × ||p1 - p4||

  where ||·|| denotes Euclidean distance. The average of the left and right EAR
  values is used as the final EAR for the frame.

Step 6 — Drowsiness Decision: If the average EAR falls below a threshold (default
0.25) for a sustained number of consecutive frames (default 20 frames, ≈0.7 s at
30 fps), a drowsiness alert is triggered. This frame-counting approach prevents
false positives from normal blinks, which typically last only 3–5 frames.

Step 7 — Yawn Detection: The 20 landmark points corresponding to the mouth region
(points 49–68) are extracted. A simplified Mouth Aspect Ratio (MAR) is computed.
When MAR exceeds a threshold (default 0.60), a yawn is recorded.

Step 8 — Alarm and Logging: When drowsiness is detected, a background thread
plays a looped audio alarm using pygame. The alarm stops automatically when the
EAR returns above the threshold. Events are appended to a CSV log file with full
timestamps.

Step 9 — Frame Annotation: Eye contours, the face bounding box, the EAR value,
MAR value, blink count, and alert banners are drawn onto the frame and displayed
in a live window.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5. SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The system is divided into four Python modules, each with a single, clearly
defined responsibility:

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │                         main.py                         │
  │   Entry point. Manages the detection loop, session      │
  │   state (ear_counter, blink_counter, yawn_counter),     │
  │   alarm triggering logic, and CLI arguments.            │
  └──────────────────────┬──────────────────────────────────┘
                         │ uses
           ┌─────────────┼──────────────────┐
           ▼             ▼                  ▼
  ┌──────────────┐ ┌──────────┐  ┌──────────────────┐
  │ detector.py  │ │ alarm.py │  │    utils.py       │
  │              │ │          │  │                   │
  │  - dlib face │ │ - Thread │  │ - Draw contours   │
  │    detector  │ │   safe   │  │ - HUD / banner    │
  │  - 68-point  │ │   audio  │  │ - CSV logging     │
  │    predictor │ │   alarm  │  │ - WAV generator   │
  │  - EAR / MAR │ │          │  │                   │
  └──────────────┘ └──────────┘  └──────────────────┘

The data flow is linear: for each video frame, main.py calls detector.py to
obtain EAR and MAR values; it then applies threshold logic; if an alert is needed
it calls alarm.py; regardless of alert state it calls utils.py to annotate the
frame and (when events occur) log them.

This decoupled architecture means each module can be tested or replaced
independently. For example, the alarm module could be swapped for a CAN-bus
message without touching the detection logic.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
6. IMPLEMENTATION DETAILS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

6.1 detector.py — DrowsinessDetector Class

The DrowsinessDetector class is initialised with the path to dlib's pre-trained
shape predictor model (shape_predictor_68_face_landmarks.dat, ~100 MB). The
__init__ method creates both the HOG face detector and the shape predictor. The
main method, process_frame(frame), accepts an OpenCV BGR image, converts it to
grayscale, detects all faces, and for each face computes and returns the EAR, MAR,
landmark arrays, and face bounding rectangle. Results are returned as a list of
dictionaries to naturally support multi-face scenarios.

The eye_aspect_ratio() and mouth_aspect_ratio() functions are implemented as
module-level helpers rather than instance methods, since they are pure mathematical
operations with no dependence on model state.

6.2 alarm.py — Thread-Safe Audio Module

Using a background thread for audio is critical: audio playback (especially a
looped alarm) blocks the calling thread, which would freeze the video loop if run
synchronously. The module maintains a global flag (_alarm_playing) that the
background thread reads in a loop. When stop_alarm() is called, this flag is set
to False, and the thread exits its loop on the next iteration. A join(timeout=2)
call ensures the thread is fully terminated before the program continues.

The module implements a three-tier fallback for audio: pygame (preferred,
cross-platform, well-maintained), winsound (Windows stdlib, no install needed),
and playsound (simple but has known issues on some platforms). This makes the
system robust across different environments.

6.3 utils.py — Drawing, Logging, and WAV Generation

The drawing functions use OpenCV's drawing primitives. Eye contours are drawn as
convex hulls of the six landmark points, giving a clean, anatomically accurate
outline. The HUD banner uses a semi-transparent overlay (cv2.addWeighted) to
avoid fully obscuring the video feed behind it.

The CSV logger uses Python's built-in csv.DictWriter, which guarantees consistent
column ordering and correct quoting. The log file is opened in append mode so
logs persist across multiple sessions without being overwritten.

The generate_alarm_wav() function generates a 2-second sine wave at 880 Hz (A5)
using Python's built-in wave and struct modules — no external library is needed.
This means the project works out of the box without requiring the user to source
an audio file, while still maintaining compatibility with any external WAV file
placed at the same path.

6.4 main.py — Detection Loop and State Machine

The main loop implements a simple state machine for each of the two detectable
conditions:

  • Drowsiness: The ear_counter variable increments every frame the EAR is below
    threshold and resets when the EAR rises above it. The alert fires at the
    counter threshold. The blink counter increments when eye_was_closed transitions
    from True to False before the counter reaches the alert threshold (i.e., a
    brief closure = a blink, not drowsiness).

  • Yawning: The is_yawning flag is set on the rising edge of MAR exceeding its
    threshold and cleared on the falling edge. The yawn_counter increments only on
    the rising edge, preventing over-counting during a sustained yawn.

7. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The system was tested informally under a variety of conditions using a standard
built-in laptop webcam at approximately 28–30 fps. Key observations:

  • Under good indoor lighting (overhead fluorescent / LED), face detection was
    reliable and consistent, with facial landmarks tracked smoothly even at moderate
    head movement (±30° yaw, ±20° pitch).

  • EAR-based drowsiness detection reliably distinguished sustained eye closure
    (>0.7 s) from normal blinks (<0.2 s). The default threshold of EAR < 0.25 for
    20 consecutive frames was found to be a good balance between sensitivity and
    specificity for the tested subjects.

  • Yawn detection was effective for large, open-mouthed yawns but occasionally
    missed smaller, suppressed yawns (where the driver covers their mouth). This is
    an inherent limitation of the MAR approach.

  • Processing speed on a mid-range laptop CPU (Intel Core i5, no GPU) averaged
    approximately 26–30 fps, which is real-time by any practical definition.

  • Under low-light conditions (dim ambient illumination), face detection became
    unreliable, with the detector frequently failing to find a face. This is a
    hardware limitation of standard webcams and is addressed in Section 9.

  • The audio alarm was audible and effective in snapping attention. The looping
    behaviour ensured that a brief accidental detection did not constitute a
    meaningful alarm, while a genuine drowsiness event produced a persistent,
    escalating experience.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
8. CHALLENGES FACED
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Several technical and practical challenges were encountered during development:

  (a) Installing dlib on Windows: The dlib library requires CMake and a C++
      compiler (Visual Studio Build Tools). On Windows, this is a non-trivial setup
      step that is a common point of failure for beginners. The solution was to
      document this clearly in the README and point users to pre-built wheel files.

  (b) Audio threading: Blocking audio playback on the main thread caused the
      video loop to freeze during alarm events. The solution was to run all audio
      in a daemon thread with a shared flag for cooperative shutdown.

  (c) Blink vs. drowsiness discrimination: A normal blink lasts approximately
      150–400 ms. At 30 fps, this corresponds to 4–12 frames. Drowsiness-related
      eye closure is sustained for much longer. The frame-counter approach
      (defaulting to 20 frames ≈ 0.67 s) cleanly separates these two cases,
      though the optimal threshold is subject-specific.

  (d) Lighting variability: dlib's HOG face detector performs well under normal
      indoor lighting but degrades under extreme shadows, backlighting, or very
      low ambient light. Handling these edge cases requires infrared cameras or
      more sophisticated face detection models such as RetinaFace.

  (e) Glasses and occlusion: Heavily tinted or thick-rimmed glasses can occlude
      the eye landmarks, causing spurious drops in EAR. This was managed by
      accepting that such cases are inherent limitations of the optical approach
      and documenting them as future work (infrared or CNN-based detection).


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
9. FUTURE SCOPE
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This project establishes a solid foundation. Several meaningful extensions are
possible:

  (a) Head Pose Estimation: By fitting a 3D head model to the 68 landmarks, it
      is possible to estimate head roll, pitch, and yaw. A large nodding pitch
      angle even without extreme EAR drop is a strong indicator of microsleep.

  (b) PERCLOS Metric: The industry-standard PERCLOS (Percentage of Eye Closure)
      metric computes the proportion of time the eye is more than 80% closed over
      a rolling window. This is more statistically robust than instantaneous EAR
      and is used in clinical fatigue research.

  (c) Deep Learning Eye State Classifier: A lightweight CNN (such as MobileNetV2
      or EfficientNet-Lite) trained on an eye-region dataset could classify eyes
      as open/closed with higher accuracy under poor lighting, glasses, and varied
      ethnicities.

  (d) Infrared Camera Support: A near-infrared or active IR illumination camera
      would make the system effective in complete darkness — ideal for night
      driving, which is the highest-risk scenario.

  (e) Driver Calibration Phase: A 30-second calibration at startup could estimate
      the driver's personal EAR baseline, making the threshold adaptive rather
      than fixed.

  (f) Vehicle Integration: The alarm output could be sent over a CAN-bus interface
      to the vehicle's infotainment system, enabling seat vibration, audio
      announcements, or even automatic brake assist.

  (g) Cloud Fleet Monitoring: For commercial transport operators, session logs
      could be streamed to a cloud dashboard, enabling fleet managers to identify
      chronically fatigued drivers.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
10. CONCLUSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This project successfully demonstrates a real-time driver drowsiness detection
system built entirely from pre-trained components, with no requirement for custom
dataset collection or model training. The system leverages the well-established
Eye Aspect Ratio metric applied to dlib's 68-point facial landmarks to detect
sustained eye closure, and augments it with Mouth Aspect Ratio-based yawn
detection for a more comprehensive picture of driver alertness.

The implementation is modular, well-documented, and designed to be maintainable.
It runs comfortably in real time on commodity hardware and provides immediate,
actionable feedback to the driver through an audio alarm. Supplementary features
— blink counting, yawn counting, and timestamped CSV logging — add analytical
value for post-trip review.

From a broader perspective, this project illustrates the power of computer vision
as a practical engineering tool. Technologies that required multi-million-dollar
research grants a decade ago are now accessible as open-source libraries that a
student can install with a single command. The challenge, then, is not access to
technology but creativity in its application — and preventing drowsy driving is an
application that genuinely saves lives.

It is hoped that this system, even in its current student-project form, serves as
a clear proof of concept that can inspire further development toward production-
grade driver monitoring systems.
